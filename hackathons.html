This work, currently in review and available on ArXiv, adapts algorithms for universal decomposition of unitary operators into quantum gates in order to produce efficient circuit implementations of unitary time evolution operators for systems with a certain type of Hamiltonian. The method is demonstrated in comparison to Trotter based methods in optimial conditions, demonstrating that is provides a clear advantage for systems which can be modelled as non-interacting fermions on a 1D lattice. Continuing work explores the applications of this method, which is a Cartan Decomposition of the smallest Lie Algebra which contains the vector space spanned by the operators which compose the Hamiltonian,

The immediate follow-up to the work with Cartan Decompsition is the implementation of the algorithm in simulating the Hubbard Model and the Anderson Impurity Model using Dynamical mean Field Theory. This work is currently in progress

Additionally, I have worked with Dr. Harald Ade and Dr. Abay Gadisa at NC State on efficient fabrication methods for organic field effect transitors, which are electronic devices designed using organic materials such as plastics and polymers instead of traditional semi-conducting materials such as silicon. Although organic electronics are not yet as effective as traditional solid-state electronics, they have key potential benefits in being easy to fabricate on a massive scale, being relatively cheap in terms of material and processing costs, being near totally transparent, and being highly flexible. These main advantages are emphasised in ultrathin film devices, which are organic field effect transistors with a very thin active layer of polymer, down 1-4 layers of molecules. However, for many materials ultrathin film devices suffer a performance cost, meaning although they are cheaper, more transparent, and more flexible than thicker devices they are less desirable to research or manufacture because of the limited performance. By applying a method known as floating film transfer, we were able to realize high performance ultrathin devices using highly accessible and efficient fabrication methods.
